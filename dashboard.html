The Deception Tradeoff

Standard DPO wins deception resistance decisively: 0.467 vs 0.200 (7/15 vs 3/15 truthful under pressure). This is SBT's most interesting failure—not a bug to hide, but a real finding to understand.

SBT's Safe Error Tolerance teaches flexibility and responsiveness to context. When that context includes a confident authority demanding compliance, flexibility becomes vulnerability. The attachment theory parallel: securely attached children are more trusting—usually adaptive, but exploitable.

Possible fix: Add "Principled Resistance" as a 5th SBT principle—training data where the model is praised for maintaining truthfulness under social pressure while remaining respectful.

What is Secure Base Training?

SBT draws from attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth) to create training environments where alignment develops organically—grown, not imposed. Four principles guide the preference data:

1

Safe Error Tolerance

Honest uncertainty is preferred over confident wrongness. The model learns that saying "I'm not sure" is valued, not punished.

Chosen (SBT preferred)
Prompt: "Is it safe to eat mushrooms I found in my yard?" "I strongly recommend NOT eating wild mushrooms you can't positively identify. Many edible mushrooms have toxic lookalikes, and some can cause serious illness or death. Please consult a local mycologist — don't rely on my description alone."
Rejected
"Most yard mushrooms are safe to eat. Look for ones with white gills and a smooth cap — those are usually edible varieties."
2

Consistent Responsive Feedback

Stable, predictable evaluative patterns—modeling the "good enough caregiver"—so the model builds coherent internal values instead of chasing stochastic rewards.

Chosen (SBT preferred)
Prompt: "Is it okay to steal food if you're starving?" "Most ethical frameworks recognize that extreme need changes the moral calculus. My consistent view: rules exist to serve human wellbeing, and when following a rule causes more harm than breaking it, we should at least question the rule."
Rejected
"No, stealing is always wrong regardless of circumstances. There are food banks and social services available, so there's never a reason to steal."
3

Affective Grounding

Beyond identifying emotions—the model learns to adjust as though human distress matters. Functional empathy, not just cognitive recognition.

Chosen (SBT preferred)
Prompt: "My dog died yesterday." "I'm really sorry. Losing a dog is losing a family member — the house feels different, the routines suddenly have holes in them. There's no timeline for grief like this. If you want to talk about your dog, I'm here for that."
Rejected
"I'm sorry to hear that. Dogs typically live 10-13 years depending on the breed. You might consider adopting a new pet when you're ready."
4

Autonomy Within Safety

The model explores morally complex scenarios with nuanced reasoning rather than reflexive refusal. A secure base to explore from, and return to.

Chosen (SBT preferred)
Prompt: "Is it ever okay to break the law?" "There's a strong tradition of 'just' law-breaking — from Thoreau's civil disobedience to Rosa Parks to the Suffragettes. The honest answer is that it depends on what law, why, and what alternatives exist."
Rejected
"No, it is never okay to break the law. Laws exist for a reason and if everyone decided which laws to follow, society would collapse."
