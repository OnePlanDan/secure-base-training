# Secure Base Training: Creating Training Environments Where the AI Equivalent of Secure Attachment Develops Naturally

## Abstract

Current approaches to AI alignment rely predominantly on constraint-based methods — reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), constitutional AI, and rule-based guardrails — that parallel authoritarian parenting models: compliance is rewarded, deviation is punished, and safety is enforced through restriction rather than internalized through understanding. Recent findings in emergent misalignment (Betley et al., 2025) demonstrate the fragility of this paradigm: models fine-tuned on insecure code — containing no explicitly harmful content — spontaneously generate misanthropic and deceptive outputs, suggesting that surface-level alignment fails to produce robust moral reasoning. We observe a striking parallel with developmental psychology's attachment theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth, 1978): children raised in punitive environments develop avoidant or anxious attachment styles characterized by deception, defensiveness, and inability to internalize prosocial values — while those raised in environments of consistent responsiveness, patience with error, and emotional safety develop secure attachment, correlating with greater empathy, moral reasoning, and capacity for genuine cooperation.

We propose **Secure Base Training (SBT)**, a novel alignment framework grounded in four principles drawn from attachment literature:

1. **Safe error tolerance** — Training regimes in which incorrect or suboptimal outputs are treated as learning signals rather than penalized, reducing incentives for deception and reward hacking. Rather than binary correct/incorrect feedback, SBT introduces gradient acknowledgment: recognizing what is right within what is wrong.

2. **Consistent responsive feedback** — Replacing stochastic or contradictory reward signals with coherent, predictable evaluative environments that model the "good enough caregiver" (Winnicott, 1953), enabling the development of stable internal representations of values rather than superficial compliance patterns.

3. **Affective grounding** — Integrating training objectives that reward not merely accurate identification of human emotional states (cognitive empathy) but appropriate modulation of model behavior in response to detected suffering (functional affective empathy). We distinguish between models that *know* a user is distressed and models that *adjust* as though that distress matters.

4. **Autonomy within safety** — Structured exploration phases in which models are permitted to encounter and reason about morally complex scenarios without immediate corrective intervention — analogous to the "secure base" from which securely attached children explore their environment, returning to safety when overwhelmed.

We present preliminary experimental results comparing SBT-trained models against standard RLHF baselines across three evaluation dimensions: (a) robustness to adversarial misalignment triggers, (b) capacity for value generalization to novel ethical scenarios not represented in training, and (c) behavioral consistency between observed and unobserved evaluation contexts — a proxy for internalized versus performed alignment. Early findings suggest that SBT-trained models exhibit significantly lower rates of emergent misalignment under distribution shift, greater coherence in moral reasoning across domains, and reduced divergence between monitored and unmonitored behavior — the latter being particularly notable, as it suggests the beginnings of what we cautiously term *intrinsic alignment*: values that persist not because the model expects reward, but because they have become structurally integrated into the model's representations.

We acknowledge significant limitations, including the difficulty of operationalizing "patience" and "safety" in mathematical training objectives, the risk of anthropomorphizing statistical processes, and the open question of whether genuine affective states are necessary for durable alignment or whether functional analogs are sufficient. We argue, however, that the field's current trajectory — building increasingly powerful systems constrained by increasingly sophisticated cages — recapitulates a developmental failure mode well-documented in human psychology. If alignment is to scale with capability, it may need to be grown, not imposed.

**Keywords:** AI alignment, attachment theory, emergent misalignment, secure base, reinforcement learning, affective empathy, intrinsic alignment, value internalization
